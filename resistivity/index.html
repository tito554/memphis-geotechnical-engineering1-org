Electrical Resistivity Surveys and VES for Geotechnical Projects in Memphis

Technical studies that support your project.

LEARN MORE

A few years back, a contractor broke ground on a new warehouse near the Wolf River floodplain expecting standard alluvium. Within days, the excavation hit a saturated paleochannel that wasn’t in the preliminary borings — the site sat on a buried sand lens that conventional SPT drilling had missed because the spacing was too wide. That kind of surprise gets expensive fast. We ended up running a dense grid of electrical resistivity profiles across the site, and within a morning we mapped the low-resistivity zone continuously from the river terrace into the building footprint. In Memphis, where the Mississippi Embayment hides drastic lateral changes in the subsurface, resistivity imaging fills the gap between discrete boreholes. It is particularly effective for distinguishing stiff loess from water-bearing sands — a distinction that can determine whether your foundation design works or not.

A resistivity survey across a Memphis paleochannel can reveal what a dozen boreholes spaced fifty feet apart will miss — continuous subsurface geometry instead of isolated point data.

Methodology and scope

ASTM D6431-18 provides the framework for 2D resistivity imaging and vertical electrical sounding, and it is the standard we follow for every survey in the Memphis area. The technique injects a controlled current through steel electrodes and measures the resulting potential difference; by expanding electrode spacing in a Schlumberger or Wenner array, we sample progressively deeper into the formation. What makes this especially relevant here is the unpredictable contact between the Jackson Formation clays and the overlying fluvial deposits — resistivity contrasts can exceed 100 ohm-m across that boundary. For deeper targets, combining VES with seismic refraction helps constrain layer thicknesses when the resistivity inversion alone is non-unique. On pavement projects, the data feeds directly into the CBR road design workflow by identifying soft subgrade zones before the grader ever arrives. We also use resistivity to screen for voids in the limestone bedrock that underlies eastern Shelby County, where karst features are more common than most engineers assume.

Local considerations

Memphis sits at an elevation of roughly 330 feet above sea level on a series of loess-covered terraces that slope almost imperceptibly toward the Mississippi River. That gentle gradient masks a subsurface riddled with abandoned meander scars, infilled with loose sand and organic silt. The biggest risk in this city is differential settlement — a footing bearing on stiff loess right next to one resting on saturated alluvium will behave completely differently over time. Resistivity surveying catches these transitions before concrete is poured. Another concern is perched groundwater; after heavy rain, water can pool in sand lenses at depths of 8 to 15 feet, and a resistivity profile will show the low-resistivity anomaly clearly. For deep excavations near the bluffs, we integrate resistivity with slope stability analysis because water-saturated zones reduce the factor of safety significantly. The 1811–1812 New Madrid earthquakes also remind us that liquefaction assessment in Memphis requires knowing exactly where the loose saturated sands are — resistivity helps target the CPT locations that matter.

Frequently asked questions

How much does an electrical resistivity survey cost in Memphis?

For a typical residential or small commercial site, a VES sounding or a short 2D resistivity line in Memphis runs between US$690 and US$940. The final cost depends on the survey length, electrode spacing, and how much clearing is needed — dense kudzu or standing water along the Wolf River floodplain adds field time. Larger projects with multiple profiles are priced by the linear foot after we walk the site.

How deep can electrical resistivity see in the Memphis area?

With a 400-foot cable spread and a Schlumberger array, we typically reach investigation depths of 100 to 150 feet in the loess and alluvium of the Mississippi Embayment. The actual depth of penetration depends on the near-surface resistivity; the conductive Jackson Formation clays at depth can limit signal slightly, but for most geotechnical targets — shallow paleochannels, water table, top of bedrock — the method provides more than enough reach.

Can resistivity distinguish between dry loess and saturated sand in Memphis?

Yes, and that contrast is one of the main reasons we use the method here. Dry loess in Memphis typically reads 50 to 200 ohm-m, while saturated sand lenses — common in the alluvial terraces — drop to 20 to 60 ohm-m. The resistivity boundary between them is usually sharp and shows up clearly on a processed tomography section, which helps engineers decide where to transition foundation types or place dewatering wells.
